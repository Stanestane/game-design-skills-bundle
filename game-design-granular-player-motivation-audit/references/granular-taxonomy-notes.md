# Granular Player Motivation Taxonomy - Source Notes

Use this file as the compact source-faithful reference for `game-design-granular-player-motivation-audit`.

## Motivation themes

### 1. Personal Achievement
- Challenge myself
- Feel a sense of accomplishment
- Improve my skills for the game, play, or real life

### 2. In-Game Achievement
- Advance / go forward to the next level
- Collect all resources, characters, or items available
- Gain mastery of new situations and challenges

### 3. Cognitive
- Improve my memory
- Solve an intellectual challenge
- Prevent mental / cognitive decline
- Keep my mind sharp
- Learn new things
- Feel smart

### 4. Emotional Immersion
- Feel completely engrossed in a world / story / game
- Nurture or care for someone or something
- Have an emotional experience
- Feel a sense of story
- Have emotional ownership
- Feel like I am an integral part of a story
- Be someone else

### 5. Creation + Exploration
- Create a world of my own
- Use my imagination
- Freely explore the way I want
- Create things I can call my own
- Explore new environments and other worlds

### 6. Affiliation
- Connect with friends and family
- Be part of the in-crowd or popular group
- Belong to something bigger
- Make new friends
- Be part of a team
- Feel accepted / supported by others
- Develop social skills
- Fit in

### 7. Independence
- Create a different life I want to live
- Be in control of something
- Have an experience and not be judged
- Break rules
- Do something I cannot do in real life

### 8. Status + Rank
- Obtain the highest rank
- Be the best
- Be better than others
- Gain status / prestige among competitors

### 9. Leadership + Power
- Be in charge / lead others
- Show I have power or influence
- Gain prestige among my group
- Dominate others / opponents

### 10. Rewards
- Earn real money / currency
- Earn free rewards or prizes

## Archetypes and shares

- **Steady Advancer** - 17%
- **Curious Solver** - 14%
- **Competitive Achiever** - 13%
- **Imaginative Creator** - 11%
- **Strategic Leader** - 11%
- **Immersed Storywriter** - 10%
- **Reward Seeker** - 9%
- **Passionate Belonger** - 7%
- **Category Enthusiast** - 7%

## Archetype notes

### Steady Advancer (17%)
**Who they are**
- All about simple, straightforward progress
- Often play daily but do not strongly identify as gamers
- Use games for accomplishment and fleeting "me-time"
- Turned off by games demanding too much mental attention or competition
- Often loyal to familiar puzzle games with short-burst level satisfaction

**Distinctive motivations**
- Advance to the next level
- Feel a sense of accomplishment
- Challenge myself
- Obtain the highest rank

**Key insights**
- Picky and quick to delete games that do not hook them
- Gaming is not a life priority
- Often multitask while playing, so complexity and heavy immersion are liabilities

**Design cues**
- Simple progression
- Quick sessions
- Reliability and familiarity
- Low cognitive overhead
- Clear next-step payoff

### Curious Solver (14%)
**Who they are**
- Inquisitive players seeking cognitive improvement
- Use games to keep the mind sharp and feel smart
- Often do not identify as gamers
- Can rationalize play as self-improvement rather than mere entertainment

**Distinctive motivations**
- Prevent mental or cognitive decline
- Improve memory
- Keep mind sharp
- Solve intellectual challenge
- Challenge myself
- Learn new things
- Feel smart
- Improve skills
- Feel a sense of accomplishment
- Gain mastery of new situations and challenges
- Connect with friends and family

**Not important**
- Leadership + Power
- Independence
- Rewards
- Creation + Exploration
- Emotional Immersion

**Key insights**
- Self-improvement is a broad life theme
- Many grew up with board games
- Cultural differences matter: memory, challenge, or mental sharpness may dominate by region

**Design cues**
- Escalating challenge
- Word, puzzle, or brain-training flavor
- Performance tracking
- Friendly competition with known people
- Mentally active, not just busywork

### Competitive Achiever (13%)
**Who they are**
- Love competition and leaderboard domination
- Seek bragging rights, especially against friends and family
- Prefer challenging games they can quickly master
- Story is useful mainly as context, not as emotional destination

**Distinctive motivations**
- Gain status / prestige among competitors
- Show off and have bragging rights
- Be better than others
- Dominate others / opponents
- Be the best
- Obtain the highest rank
- Connect with friends and family

**Not important**
- Emotional connection to a story
- Feeling integral to a story
- Making new friends

**Key insights**
- Want to win the right way; dislike cheats or purchased advantage
- Emotional immersion rates low despite some nominal interest in story context
- Late adopters for eSports relative to Strategic Leaders and Passionate Belongers

**Design cues**
- Fair skill competition
- Ladders, ranks, comparison, mastery proof
- High clarity of victory conditions
- Recognition without pay-to-win contamination

### Imaginative Creator (11%)
**Who they are**
- Want to create their own world and make their vision real
- Use games for expression, freedom, control, and creativity
- Enjoy shaping layout, inhabitants, exploration, and personal narrative
- Experience immersion through authorship and world-building

**Distinctive motivations**
- Create a world of my own
- Nurture or care for someone or something
- Freely explore the way I want
- Create things I can call my own
- Use my imagination
- Explore new environments and other worlds
- Collect all resources, characters, or items available
- Do something I cannot do in real life
- Create a different life I want to live
- Be someone else
- Be in control of something in my life
- Feel completely engrossed in a world / story / game

**Not important**
- Show off and have bragging rights
- Gain status / prestige among competitors
- Dominate others / opponents
- Be in charge / lead others
- Be the best
- Obtain the highest rank
- Be better than others

**Key insights**
- Control is central
- Immersion comes through making the world theirs
- Real-life creative interests often align with this segment

**Design cues**
- Customization depth
- Nurturing loops
- Sandbox structures
- Ownership and curation
- Exploration with expressive freedom

### Strategic Leader (11%)
**Who they are**
- Skilled gamers proud of playing smart and leading others to victory
- Highly social and highly competitive
- Value outplaying and outthinking opponents
- Will teach others if it improves team success
- Research tactics and invent winning approaches

**Distinctive motivations**
- Gain status / prestige among competitors
- Feel supported by others
- Be the best
- Be in charge / lead others
- Show off and have bragging rights
- Feel a sense of ownership
- Be better than others
- Learn new things
- Obtain the highest rank
- Dominate others / opponents
- Make new friends
- Be part of a team
- Feel smart
- Improve my skills
- Gain mastery of new situations and challenges

**Not important**
- Have an experience and not be judged
- Nurture and care for someone or something
- Prevent mental or cognitive decline

**Key insights**
- Strong gamer identity
- Blends cognitive, competitive, and social motives
- Most likely to form new relationships for high-level play

**Design cues**
- Tactical depth
- Leadership roles
- Team coordination
- Strategy research/optimization loops
- Recognition for group impact and mastery

### Immersed Storywriter (10%)
**Who they are**
- Want control over a protagonist's actions and fate
- Seek complete engrossment in the game experience
- Need freedom, control, and emotional connection to characters
- Often see games as meaningful art, not just pastime

**Distinctive motivations**
- Have an emotional connection to a story
- Feel completely engrossed in a world / story / game
- Feel like I am an integral part of a story
- Explore new environments and other worlds
- Be someone else
- Freely explore the way I want
- Do something I cannot do in real life
- Have an emotional response to an experience
- Create a different life I want to live
- Create a world of my own

**Not important**
- Dominate others / opponents
- Earn real money / currency
- Develop social skills
- Feel supported by others

**Key insights**
- Treat games as an art form
- Absorption matters even more than narrow story formalism
- Strong narrative/world coherence can make even smaller-scale games compelling

**Design cues**
- Emotional stakes
- Rich worldbuilding
- Character interaction
- atmospheric exploration
- choices with consequences
- identity and role inhabitation

### Reward Seeker (9%)
**Who they are**
- Motivated primarily by prizes, virtual or physical
- Want reward totals to rise
- Tolerate luck if payout potential is attractive
- Prefer simple games with frequent rewards and dreams of big wins

**Distinctive motivations**
- Earn real money / currency
- Earn free rewards or prizes
- Challenge myself

**Not important**
- Cognitive
- Emotional Immersion
- Creation + Exploration
- Affiliation
- Independence
- Status + Rank
- Leadership + Power

**Key insights**
- Treat in-game currency as having real value
- Often ritualize play around harvesting rewards across multiple games
- Frequently casual in genre depth even when on PC

**Design cues**
- Visible payout moments
- Daily bonuses and jackpot logic
- Currency accumulation
- reward cadence
- simple low-friction loops

### Passionate Belonger (7%)
**Who they are**
- Defined mainly by belonging, fit, support, and social participation
- Often drawn to team identity, community, and shared play
- Less interested in bragging rights or being the best than in being part of something

**Distinctive motivations**
- Fit in
- Be part of the in-crowd or popular group
- Make new friends
- Develop social skills
- Feel supported by others
- Be part of a team
- Connect with friends and family
- Feel a sense of ownership
- Break the rules
- Belong to something big

**Not important**
- Show off and have bragging rights
- Be better than others
- Dominate others / opponents
- Be the best

**Design cues**
- Team play
- Supportive interaction
- chat, gifting, coordination, co-op
- community and guild structures
- shared events and rituals
- social warmth rather than pure comparison

### Category Enthusiast (7%)
**Source note**
- "Cannot design for or market to these players"

**Interpretation**
- Treat as a residual category, not a clean motivation archetype
- Use it as a warning against over-attributing appeal to universal motives when the real driver may simply be genre/category loyalty
- If a feature only works for people already invested in the category, that may be genre capture rather than broad motivational fit

## Practical interpretation rules

1. Use **motivation themes** to explain the forces inside the design.
2. Use **archetypes** to predict audience fit, exclusion, and likely reception.
3. Pay special attention to each segment's **"What Is Not Important"** list; it often explains why a feature fails even when it seems generally well-made.
4. Distinguish **Competitive Achiever** from **Strategic Leader**:
   - Competitive Achiever wants status, rank, and earned superiority
   - Strategic Leader wants that too, but also team command, tactical depth, and coordinated mastery
5. Distinguish **Imaginative Creator** from **Immersed Storywriter**:
   - Imaginative Creator wants authorship, control, nurturing, and self-expression
   - Immersed Storywriter wants absorption, emotional connection, and inhabiting a meaningful world/story
6. Distinguish **Passionate Belonger** from **Strategic Leader**:
   - Passionate Belonger wants fit, support, and group identity
   - Strategic Leader wants high-level coordinated success, influence, and prestige
7. Use **Reward Seeker** carefully when auditing monetization or event design; this segment can create deceptive engagement signals because frequent claims do not equal broad emotional appeal.
