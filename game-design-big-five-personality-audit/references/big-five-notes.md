# Big Five / OCEAN Notes for Game Design

Use this file as a compact reference for `game-design-big-five-personality-audit`.

## Important warning

Use OCEAN as a **design lens**, not a fake diagnostic tool.

Do not claim a game is "for agreeable people" or that a player definitively has a trait from one behavior. The useful question is:

- which trait expressions does this design feel comfortable for?
- which trait expressions does it reward?
- which trait expressions does it frustrate, exhaust, or repel?

This is about **fit**, not labeling humans.

## The Big Five

### 1. Openness to Experience
Associated with:
- curiosity
- novelty seeking
- imagination
- tolerance for ambiguity
- experimentation
- interest in aesthetics, symbolism, systems, and unusual combinations

In game terms, high-openness players often tolerate or enjoy:
- discovery
- unusual mechanics
- expressive customization
- emergent systems
- lore, symbolism, and weirdness
- experimentation without one obvious optimal path

Lower-openness expressions often prefer:
- familiarity
- clearer conventions
- lower ambiguity
- more straightforward goals
- less demand for novelty-processing

### 2. Conscientiousness
Associated with:
- planning
- discipline
- order
- delayed gratification
- completion orientation
- preference for structure and legibility

In game terms, high-conscientiousness expressions often tolerate or enjoy:
- checklists
- optimization
- routine loops
- scheduling
- collection completion
- efficient progression
- legible systems with clear payoff

Lower-conscientiousness expressions often prefer:
- improvisation
- lower maintenance burden
- less obligation
- looser structures
- lower punishment for inconsistency

### 3. Extraversion
Associated with:
- social stimulation
- assertiveness
- energetic engagement
- visible activity
- responsiveness to social presence and interaction volume

In game terms, high-extraversion expressions often tolerate or enjoy:
- lively multiplayer spaces
- team coordination
- social visibility
- leadership opportunities
- chat, clubs, guilds, group rituals
- high-arousal play

Lower-extraversion expressions often prefer:
- solo autonomy
- quieter interaction
- asynchronous or low-pressure social systems
- lower noise and lower social demand

### 4. Agreeableness
Associated with:
- cooperation
- empathy
- harmony preference
- prosociality
- dislike of harsh conflict or exploitation

In game terms, high-agreeableness expressions often tolerate or enjoy:
- co-op
- helping systems
- gifting
- support roles
- warm community structures
- low-toxicity interaction

Lower-agreeableness expressions may tolerate or enjoy:
- sharper competition
- adversarial play
- trash talk
- dominance games
- exploitative negotiation
- aggressive status contests

### 5. Neuroticism (Emotional Volatility / Sensitivity)
Associated with:
- stress sensitivity
- loss aversion
- self-doubt
- emotional reactivity
- stronger response to uncertainty, failure, punishment, and social judgment

In game terms, higher-neuroticism expressions may be more affected by:
- punishing failure
- unclear systems
- high social evaluation
- humiliation risk
- harsh FOMO
- chaotic variance
- irreversible mistakes

But they may also respond strongly to:
- safety
- reassurance
- predictable recovery
- comfort loops
- low-stakes mastery
- emotionally soothing structure

Lower-neuroticism expressions often tolerate or enjoy:
- harsher setbacks
- ambiguity
- competitive scrutiny
- emotional roughness
- risk-taking under pressure

## Practical interpretation rules

### Openness
Ask:
- does the design reward curiosity and experimentation?
- does it punish deviation from the obvious path?
- is the experience creatively or aesthetically rich?
- is novelty refreshing or merely confusing?

### Conscientiousness
Ask:
- does the design reward planning, order, and consistency?
- does it become a maintenance job?
- are obligations motivating or exhausting?
- is the system legible enough to support disciplined play?

### Extraversion
Ask:
- does the design come alive around other people?
- are there visible, energetic, socially stimulating moments?
- does it over-demand social participation?
- is there enough to do alone if the player wants low-noise play?

### Agreeableness
Ask:
- does the design support helping, trust, and prosocial coordination?
- does it create conflict, betrayal, pressure, or cruelty?
- does it reward social warmth or social predation?
- how safe does interaction feel?

### Neuroticism
Ask:
- how punishing, humiliating, uncertain, or stressful is the design?
- are failure and recovery emotionally survivable?
- how much FOMO, performance pressure, or scarcity anxiety is present?
- does the design create comfort, reassurance, and predictable recovery where needed?

## Common design patterns by trait fit

### High Openness fit
- sandboxes
- expressive builders
- unusual systemic games
- discovery-rich worlds
- non-obvious interactions

### High Conscientiousness fit
- planners
- completionists
- optimization loops
- routine-driven live service structures
- efficient progression systems

### High Extraversion fit
- loud multiplayer
- club/guild-heavy structures
- social visibility
- high-energy events
- leadership roles

### High Agreeableness fit
- helping systems
- co-op
- gifting
- warm community rituals
- supportive roles

### Lower Agreeableness fit
- dominance PvP
- sabotage
- cutthroat economy or diplomacy
- trash-talk tolerated environments

### Higher Neuroticism fit
- comfort games
- low-stakes mastery
- reversible experimentation
- predictable recovery loops
- clear guidance

### Lower Neuroticism fit
- high-pressure competition
- punishing failure loops
- uncertainty-rich systems
- public ranking scrutiny

## Anti-pattern warning

Do not use this framework to make fake psychometric claims.
Use it to reason about:
- tolerance
- emotional cost
- social comfort
- cognitive appetite
- structure preference
- stimulation preference
